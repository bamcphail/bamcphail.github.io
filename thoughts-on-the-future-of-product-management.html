Thoughts on the Future of Product Management
March 2026  |  ← All blogs

Product management is at an inflection point. The role has spent the last decade accumulating responsibilities — roadmaps, metrics, stakeholder alignment, discovery, delivery — until it became something of a catch-all for anything that didn't fit cleanly into engineering or design. That era is ending.

The PMs who will thrive in the next decade are not the ones who master the process. Process is being automated. The tools that once required a product manager to translate between customer and engineer — the Jira tickets, the PRDs, the sprint ceremonies — are collapsing in on themselves. AI can write a spec from a conversation. Engineers can prototype from a prompt. The coordination tax is going down.

What remains is judgment. Not judgment about which feature to build, but judgment about which problem is worth solving. That's harder to automate, and it's also harder to fake. For years it was possible to have a successful PM career by being good at process and reasonably good at communication. That path is narrowing fast.

The PMs I most respect today are people who have genuine domain expertise in something — not just product craft. They understand the underlying economics of their market. They can read a balance sheet and know what it means for product strategy. They have relationships with customers that go deeper than a quarterly NPS survey. They have opinions that would survive contact with someone who really knows the space.

There's also a shift happening in where product thinking lives. For a long time, product management was centralized — a specialized function sitting between engineering and the business. That model made sense when building software was expensive and coordination was the bottleneck. Now building is cheap and fast, and the bottleneck is understanding what to build. That understanding can't live in a single team. It has to be distributed.

The future of product management probably looks less like a discipline and more like a capability — one that's embedded throughout an organization rather than housed in a function. The best product managers will own that transition rather than resist it.
